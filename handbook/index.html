The mental model — four resources

Entities are the catalog of nouns; the other three record what's true about them.

Entities

The catalog of parties/assets — companies, funds, holdings, individuals, accounts. The nouns every other record points to.

Drives: Identity, type & attributes (currency, domicile, legal form); how holdings group.

Transactions

A real flow from one entity to another on a date — money and/or an instrument changing hands.

Drives: Cost basis, unit counts, cash in/out, commitments.

Measurements

A spot, as-of fact about an entity or instrument — most importantly a fair-value mark.

Drives: Current value, per-unit price, company KPIs.

Events

A loose, informative timeline entry. Narrative only.

Drives: Nothing computed — display context.

Every transaction, measurement and event references an entity by its slug (subject_id / object_id). The entity supplies the name, type and currency you see; the other three supply what happened and what it's worth.

The golden rule. Entities are the catalog everything references; computed numbers come from transactions and measurements; event information are never summed up. A holding's cost is a story about flows/transactions (what was paid); its value is a story about marks/measurements (what it's worth now). They're computed independently and shown side by side.

The transaction — a flow with two legs

A transaction is an exchange — one leg for what's given, one for what's received.

A transaction is a dated exchange between two parties — the subject (whose books we're on, the holder) and the object (the counterparty / asset) — tagged with a type such as investment, distribution, exit or write_off. It's recorded as two legs: the outflow (what the subject gives) and the inflow (what it gets back). Each leg carries its own type and amount.

What the subject receives — usually an instrument, with the amount as its unit / share count.

Outflow is usually cash and inflow usually an instrument — but not always. In a currency swap both legs are cash: you give one currency on the outflow and receive another (inflow_type = cash) on the inflow. The legs are simply given vs received, not fixed to money vs instrument.
Example Crestline Holdings pays €75,000 and receives 3,000 Series A shares in Helios Energy — booked as subject → object, type investment.

Sign convention: direction lives in the sign of outflow_amount — a distribution is cash coming back, a negative outflow, never moved to the inflow leg. EUR rule: a leg's EUR value is its reporting amount when present, else the native amount only if the leg is already EUR, else unknown — shown as a dash, never coerced to 0.

The measurement — a spot fact

A single as-of observation, stored apart from flows. Valuations and KPIs live here.

One row shape covers two cases, told apart by instrument_type: when it's set the row is an instrument valuation (a fair-value mark for one share class); when it's null the row is a company KPI (revenue, EBITDA, headcount, …).

How a value attaches to a holding
A valuation finds its holding by matching the same instrument on both sides:
transaction (object_id, inflow_type) == measurement (subject_id, instrument_type)
Gotcha. A mark whose instrument_type doesn't match any transaction's inflow_type on that issuer silently fails to attach — the position then shows at cost. When a valuation isn't showing up, check this match first.

Methodology — Cost basis

The signed sum of the money legs of the flows that build a position.

Only real position flows count toward cost — buys add, cash-back nets down. Marks, promises and forecasts don't.

Included

investment, capital_call, distribution, dividend, interest, exit, transfer — each with its natural sign.

Excluded
  • valuation — a mark, not a flow.
  • commitment — promised, not paid in.
  • write_off — keeps cost; value drops via a mark.
  • status = planned — a forecast.
Cost basis is historical and sticky: a valuation never changes it, and a write-off never erases it. You always see what was paid alongside what it's worth.

Methodology — Value

Measurement-led: the latest mark, or the cost basis when there isn't one.

latest mark

The most recent fair_value for the position's (issuer, instrument) pair → count × (value ÷ units), tagged val · date. Unit-less instruments use the total.

at cost

No measurement yet? The position is shown at its cost basis and labelled at cost.

A genuine 0 mark is a real value, not "missing" — a written-off position shows €0, both in the instruments table and the header Fair Value.

Transaction types — effect on a holding

The type tells the engine what the flow does. The instrument is a free label.

investment
Cash out, instrument in. Adds to cost and count; feeds Total Investment.
capital_call
Cash drawn into a fund commitment. Adds to cost (paid-in).
commitment
A signed promise, no cash yet. Not cost — tracked as committed capital.
distribution / dividend / interest
Cash back to the holder (negative outflow). Nets cost down.
exit
Sale / liquidation; cash in, units out. Nets count and cost down.
transfer
In-kind move of instruments, no cash. Moves count between vehicles.
write_off
Impairment to zero. Keeps cost basis; value drops via a fair_value = 0 mark.
fee / invoice
A service charge — a cash flow, not a position.

Write-offs & impairments

The money was really spent, but the position is now worthless. That splits across two resources.

write_off transaction

Records that the impairment happened. Preserves the cost basis — neither adds to nor subtracts from cost.

fair_value = 0 measurement

On the same instrument, drives the value to 0.

Quick reference

Resources: transactions = flows measurements = marks events = narrative events are never summed
Instrument examples: Series Seed Series A SAFE Convertible Common Fund Interest Crypto … the instrument is a free label, so any class can appear
Value tags: val · date latest valuation mark at cost no mark, shown at cost basis
Zero vs dash: €0 a real recorded zero (e.g. a write-off) — not recorded — never the same thing
Money-leg sign: + pays out − receives a distribution is a negative outflow, never an inflow
